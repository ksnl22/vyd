Sankranti

Sankranti, also known as Makar Sankranti, is a major harvest festival celebrated across India, marking the Sunâ€™s transition into the zodiac sign of Capricorn (Makara Rashi) and the beginning of its northward journey, known as Uttarayan. This festival signifies the end of winter and the arrival of longer, warmer days, which is a crucial period for farmers as it signals the start of the harvest season and the ripening of winter crops. The celebration is deeply rooted in gratitude towards nature and the Sun God, Surya, for a bountiful harvest and prosperity. Rituals such as taking a holy dip in rivers, offering prayers, and acts of charity like donating sesame seeds, jaggery, grains, and clothes to the needy are considered sacred and bring good karma




UGADI

Ugadi is a significant Hindu festival celebrated primarily in the southern Indian states of Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, and Karnataka, marking the beginning of the new year according to the Hindu lunisolar calendar. The festival falls on the first day of the month of Chaitra, usually in March or April, symbolizing the start of a new era and the onset of spring, which is associated with renewal, growth, and prosperity. The name "Ugadi" is derived from the Sanskrit words yuga (age) and adi (beginning), meaning "the beginning of a new age." It is believed that on this day, Lord Brahma began the creation of the universe, making it an auspicious occasion for new beginnings and spiritual reflection.


Vinayaka Chavithi

Vinayaka Chavithi, also known as Ganesh Chaturthi, is a major Hindu festival celebrated with great devotion across Andhra Pradesh. The festival marks the birth of Lord Ganesha, the elephant-headed deity revered as the remover of obstacles and the god of new beginnings. Homes and public spaces are adorned with colorful rangolis and garlands, and clay idols of Ganesha are installed with rituals invoking his divine presence. Vijayadashami

Vijayadashami, also known as Dussehra, is a major Hindu festival celebrated across India and Nepal, symbolizing the triumph of good over evil. Observed on the tenth day of the month of Ashvin in the Hindu calendar, it marks the culmination of the nine-day Navratri festival and the end of Durga Puja. Vijayadashami is celebrated with diverse rituals and vibrant festivities throughout the country. In North India, effigies of Ravana, his brother Kumbhakarna, and son Meghanada are burned in public gatherings, accompanied by fireworks and dramatic enactments of the Ramayana (Ramlila). In the eastern regions, particularly Bengal, the festival concludes with the immersion of elaborately crafted Durga idols in rivers or seas, marking a farewell to the goddess and the end of Durga Puja. In southern India, the day is also considered auspicious for beginning new ventures or initiating children into learning, known as Vidyarambham.
